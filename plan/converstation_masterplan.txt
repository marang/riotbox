Thought for 26m 40s
Yes — and the Roland MC-202 is specifically the 1983 machine. Roland’s support material lists it as the MC-202 Micro Composer with about 2600 steps of memory, tape and DIN sync, and a mono synth section with PWM, resonant low-pass filter up to self-oscillation, sine LFO, portamento, and accent; a 1983 review also emphasized its two independent digital sequencers and both real-time and step entry. That is exactly why it should be the mono tension / acid voice inside this project, not the whole arrangement brain. 

For the Prodigy side, the useful lesson is not “copy famous loops.” Liam Howlett has described being part of the move from acid house into breakbeat culture, avoiding overused loops, rebuilding drums from snippets of live breaks, layering extra kick, using lots of reverb on drums, spending serious time on original synth tones, and constructing riffs from a few source notes rather than lifting whole phrases. He also preferred bar-by-bar variation over copy-paste repetition. 

So the right product is this:

Build a terminal groovebox called microriot
Not a “text prompt -> finished song” toy.
Not a DAW in disguise.
Not a Prodigy clone machine.

Build a live, terminal-first generative instrument that:

composes original rave / breakbeat / big-beat material in real time,

has one dedicated MC-202-inspired mono lane for bass/lead pressure,

lets you steer drops, fills, aggression, dirt, and arrangement from the keyboard or MIDI controller,

can also render deterministic full tracks from seeds.

I would assume:
standalone local app, real-time audio output, original or licensed sample material, no GUI, no browser, no DAW dependency.

The creative thesis
The sound should come from four interacting engines:

1. Break engine
Live-feeling chopped breaks, not static loops.

2. Hook engine
Short, menacing, memorable motifs that feel hand-built, not AI-mushy.

3. MC-202 lane
Mono acid / bass / lead line that supplies pressure, motion, and identity.

4. Arrangement brain
Knows when to tease, strip, slam, mutate, and come back harder.

That gives you “Prodigy energy with MC-202 blood” instead of “303 jam with some sampled drums thrown on top.”

The core design rule
Do not make the project depend on a giant neural model for the main loop.

For this use case, a better architecture is:

symbolic generation for rhythm, phrase, and arrangement,

deterministic seeded randomness for variation,

sampler / synth DSP for sound,

optional offline learning later to tune probabilities.

That keeps the app fast, playable, reproducible, and musical. It behaves like an instrument, not a slot machine.

Sound bible: what the engine must know
Give the engine a shared “style DNA” vector, something like:

aggression, break_liveness, acidness, dirt, hookiness, darkness, variation, rave_euphoria

Every lane reads from the same DNA, so the whole track moves together.

Then define 3 preset families for v1:

rave91
Bright, manic, cheeky, more rave stabs, lighter distortion, MC-202 mostly bass support.

dark94
Heavier breaks, darker intervals, stronger acid movement, more tension, more filter violence.

big97
Rockier hooks, more distortion, chunkier drums, more stop-start arrangement, 202 lane as both bass and stabbing lead.

Do not name presets after actual songs. Use eras, moods, or invented names.

The one killer macro: touch_202
This should be the signature control.

touch_202 = 0.0
The mono lane is subtle: low-end motion, occasional fills, background acid.

touch_202 = 0.3
The lane becomes a real bass identity.

touch_202 = 0.6
The lane starts grabbing attention with accent/slide phrases and filter snarls.

touch_202 = 1.0
The track is openly driven by the MC-202 personality.

Internally, that single control should modify:

bass-lane prominence,

accent density,

glide frequency,

filter envelope amount,

section count where the mono lane takes the lead,

how often riffs are doubled or answered by the 202 lane.

That is elegant, musical, and very usable in performance.

Runtime architecture
Use four threads or services:

[TUI + MIDI] ---> [control bus] ----\

[asset worker] -> [sample cache] ----/                       ^

Responsibilities
Audio thread

real-time safe,

no heap allocation,

consumes scheduled note/sample/automation events,

renders voices and FX,

outputs stereo frames.

Composer thread

generates 1-4 bars ahead,

reacts to mutations at phrase boundaries,

never blocks audio.

UI/control thread

keyboard, TUI navigation, MIDI learn, scene launch, parameter edits.

Asset worker

decodes samples,

resamples to engine rate,

slices breaks,

computes metadata,

writes cache.

Musical generation model
Use a hierarchical generator, not a flat loop generator.

Layer 1: section grammar
The track is made of sections such as:

tease -> establish -> build -> drop_a -> strip -> drop_b -> switchup -> final_hit -> exit

The arrangement brain chooses the next section by current energy, recent history, and contrast budget.

Layer 2: phrase generators
Each lane writes phrases, not endless patterns:

break lane: 1, 2, or 4 bars

MC-202 lane: 1 or 2 bar cells with mutations

hook lane: 2 or 4 bar motifs

FX lane: transitional phrases

Layer 3: micro variation
Every few bars:

remove or add ghost notes,

swap one slice,

change accent map,

open filter slightly,

mute a lane for half a bar,

throw a fill before impact.

The rule is: recognizable identity, constant motion.
Never 30 identical bars.

Use generate-score-select, not pure randomness
For basslines, breaks, and hooks:

generate a batch of candidates,

score them,

keep a few top candidates,

mutate the winner slightly,

schedule it.

Scoring functions
Groove score

syncopation inside target range,

backbeat clarity,

controlled swing.

Identity score

motif is memorable,

contour is clear,

not too bland.

Impact score

works with current section energy,

sets up drops and fills.

Novelty score

not too close to previous 16 bars,

not too close to reference fingerprints.

Restraint score

avoids overfilling the mix.

This is where “nice” comes from. Nice is not random. Nice is selected.

The MC-202 lane
This is the soul of the project.

Voice model
Build one mono subtractive synth voice with:

pulse/saw-style edge,

PWM,

resonant 24 dB low-pass behavior,

ADSR,

sine-LFO style modulation,

portamento,

accent,

legato-aware slide handling.

Sequencer model
Do two operating modes:

Pure mode

one mono lane,

stricter step logic,

memory-inspired limitations,

phrase editing feels “microcomposer-ish.”

Plus mode

modern undo,

infinite session memory,

parameter locks,

easier mutation,

better save/load.

This gives you historical flavor without forcing historical pain.

Bassline grammar
The lane should emit step objects with:

pitch,

gate length,

accent,

slide,

octave flag,

rest/tie.

The generator biases:

root and fifth for weight,

minor-third / flat-seventh / chromatic approaches for menace,

accent on structural points and offbeat surprises,

slide on selected transitions, not everywhere.

Ingenious extra
Add a battery mode as optional spice:
tiny pitch drift, small envelope inconsistency, slight instability under heavy accent.
Very subtle. More vibe than gimmick.

The break engine: Breaksmith
This is as important as the synth.

Howlett’s old production approach points toward reconstructing drums from pieces rather than accepting a stock loop whole, plus extra kick and roomy aggression on top. 

Sample ingest pipeline
When a pack is added:

decode audio,

detect BPM estimate,

detect onsets via spectral flux,

slice at transients,

classify slices loosely as kick / snare / ghost / hat / fill / wash,

compute loudness and brightness,

cache metadata.

Break graph
Represent a break as a graph of slices:

each slice knows its position, type, feel, and neighbors,

transitions preserve groove,

mutations replace only a few nodes at a time.

That gives you live-feeling recombination rather than obvious cut-up chaos.

Layering
On top of the sliced break:

optional 909-ish kick reinforcement,

snare transient layer,

room reverb send,

parallel distortion bus.

Because part of the reference recipe is exactly that extra slam and thrash.

The hook engine: RiffForge
The most ingenious move is this:

Generate riffs by resampling your own material.

Modern analysis of “Breathe”-style production emphasizes sharp or pulse-like tone sources, aggressive filtering, and backend distortion / bitcrushing, while Howlett’s own account describes constructing a riff from a few recorded notes rather than taking an existing phrase wholesale. 

So the workflow should be:

synthesize or record a tiny set of source notes,

map them into a small internal sampler,

generate a 2-bar motif,

process it through filters/distortion,

optionally resample it again,

reuse it as a new hook source.

That is very on-brand without being derivative.

For the rockier hook bus:

favor sharp waveforms, PWM-like bite, narrowed bandwidth,

add distortion, bit reduction, or ugly backend overdrive,

keep width modest,

keep the sound mono-ish enough to punch.

Session model and determinism
Every session should save:

That gives you:

This is crucial. Randomness without replay is a dead end.

Export model
The app should export:

stereo render,

stems,

MIDI,

session file,

provenance manifest,

optional sync track.

Because the original MC-202 supported tape save/load and DIN sync, a very fitting extra is MIDI clock out and a renderable sync-track export for hardware or cassette workflows. 

Release-safe design
Ship only original or licensed material. Howlett has spoken about sample clearance being slow and expensive, and modern recreations of Breathe explicitly frame the safe path as capturing the spirit while mutating the sound instead of rebuilding it note-for-note. So every render should include a provenance manifest and run through a similarity check. 

Similarity firewall
Before exporting, compute:

melodic n-gram similarity,

rhythmic fingerprint similarity,

arrangement similarity,

repeated hook contour similarity.

If a render crosses a threshold:

regenerate the offending phrase,

mutate rhythm or contour,

log the reason.

This is one of the smartest features in the whole plan. It keeps the tool powerful and releasable.

The features that make this special
touch_202 as a single macro controlling how dominant the MC-202 spirit is.

Pure / Plus modes so you can have vintage constraints without vintage misery.

Resample-rebuild workflow for original hooks.

Similarity firewall so the output stays inspired, not copied.

Sync-track export as a weirdly beautiful nod to tape-era hardware thinking.

What not to build first
Do not start with:

vocals,

cloud sync,

plugin hosting,

VST/AU version,

giant ML style-transfer model,

online collaboration,

full song lyric generator.

Those are distractions. The heart of the product is:
breaks, mono voice, hooks, arranger, terminal control.

Exit: stable jam session and deterministic replay.

Mono voice, sequencer, accent/slide logic, pure/plus modes, performance controls.
Exit: convincing bass and lead behavior.

Breaksmith
Ingest pipeline, slicing, classification, break graph, layering, drum bus FX.
Exit: drums feel alive and do not loop obviously.

RiffForge
Sampler-based motif creation, resample-rebuild, distortion/filter chain, hook scoring.
Exit: recurring hooks with character and no obvious quotations.

Arrangement brain

Exit: 5-7 minute generated tracks with narrative shape.

Performance layer
Hotkeys, scene launch, MIDI learn, quantized mutation, panic / reset behaviors.

Export and safety layer

Final verdict
This should be built as a feral terminal groovebox:
a breakbeat brain, a vicious MC-202 nerve, a hook forge that resamples its own ideas, and an arrangement engine that knows how to tease and hit.

The cleanest MVP is ruthless:
one exceptional break engine, one unforgettable MC-202 lane, one arranger that understands tension, and one terminal UI that begs to be played.

Die richtige Produktdefinition
Ich würde das nicht als „AI macht aus MP3 irgendwas Cooles“ definieren.

Ich würde es so definieren:

Ein terminal-natives Performance-Tool, das eingehende Musik analysiert, in ein musikalisches Zustandsmodell überführt und daraus live eine abgewandelte, spielbare Version baut — mit drei Geräte-Persönlichkeiten: MC-202, W-30, TR-909.

Der entscheidende Satz dabei ist: nicht Black-Box-Style-Transfer, sondern kontrollierte De-Konstruktion plus Re-Synthese.

Warum das wichtig ist:

Terminal-Form
Der Terminal-Client sollte nicht „CLI mit Logausgabe“ sein, sondern ein echtes TUI-Instrument.

Rust passt dafür sehr gut:
CPAL stellt Audio-Streams/Devices bereit, Ratatui ist explizit für moderne TUIs gedacht, Symphonia dekodiert u. a. MP3, und Rubber Band ist für Echtzeitmodus als RT-safe dokumentiert; zugleich muss man bei Rubber Band beachten, dass die Bibliothek GPL ist oder eine kommerzielle Lizenz erfordert. 

Das führt fast automatisch zu dieser Architektur:

Rust realtime core
  - audio callback
  - scheduler
  - mixer
  - device engines (202 / W30 / 909)
  - TUI
  - MIDI/HID mapping

Python analysis/AI sidecar
  - source separation
  - MIR analysis
  - embeddings
  - local LLM agent
  - candidate generation / scoring
Nicht alles in Python.
Nicht alles in Rust.
Realtime in Rust, Forschung/Modelle in Python.

Warum out-of-process so wichtig ist
Für ein professionelles Live-Tool gilt:

KI darf crashen, Audio nicht.

Analyse darf hängen, Playback nicht.

GPU darf spinnen, Scheduler nicht.

Jedes mutierende Event muss undo-bar sein.

Darum:

Audio-Engine eigener Prozess

AI/MIR Sidecar eigener Prozess

Kommunikation über lokales RPC oder Unix socket

Audio-Thread ohne Allokationen

Mutationen nur an sicheren Quantisierungsgrenzen

Das ist nicht glamourös, aber genau das trennt „cooles Prototype-Ding“ von „Bühnenwerkzeug“.

RAVE und AudioCraft: stark, aber nicht Kern von v1
Als Forschungsast sind zwei Dinge spannend:

RAVE passt hervorragend zu „Mutant Mode“, weil es als Realtime Audio Variational AutoEncoder für Timbral Transfer, latent manipulation und sogar Laptop-CPU-Livebetrieb beschrieben wird. Gleichzeitig steht die veröffentlichte Lizenz auf Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial 4.0. Für ein professionelles kommerzielles Produkt also eher Forschungszweig oder separat zu lizenzieren, nicht Kern von v1. 

AudioCraft ist ebenfalls verlockend: MusicGen, MusicGen Style und JASCO sind im Repo dokumentiert; JASCO wird dort sogar als text-to-music Modell genannt, das auf Akkorde, Melodien und Drum-Tracks konditioniert werden kann. Aber die Modellgewichte stehen laut Repo unter CC-BY-NC 4.0, und bereits MusicGen medium verlangt für Inferenz mindestens 16 GB GPU. Also: super als Labor- oder Offline-Ideenmaschine, aber nicht als Fundament eines schlanken, zuverlässigen Live-Kerns. 

Die nüchterne Produktrealität: Lizenzstrategie zuerst fest verdrahten
Das ist der Teil, den man früh entscheiden muss.

Für einen kommerziell professionellen Kern sind mehrere sehr brauchbare Bausteine lizenzseitig heikel oder nicht ideal:

Demucs ist MIT-lizenziert, aber das ursprüngliche Repo ist laut README nicht mehr aktiv gepflegt.

Basic Pitch ist Apache 2.0 und damit vergleichsweise angenehm.

madmom-Quellcode ist BSD, aber seine Modell-/Datenfiles sind CC BY-NC-SA 4.0.

Essentia selbst ist AGPLv3 bzw. proprietär lizenzierbar; die vortrainierten Modelle sind CC BY-NC-ND 4.0.

MERT-Gewichte auf Hugging Face stehen als CC-BY-NC 4.0.

AudioCraft-Gewichte sind CC-BY-NC 4.0.

RAVE ist in der veröffentlichten Form ebenfalls NonCommercial. 

Daraus folgt eine klare Architekturregel:

Jeder Analyse- oder KI-Baustein muss hinter einem austauschbaren Provider-Interface sitzen.

Also nicht Demucs fest einbauen, sondern StemProvider.
Nicht MERT fest einbauen, sondern EmbeddingProvider.
Nicht madmom fest einbauen, sondern BeatProvider.

So kannst du:

schnell prototypen,

später lizenzsauber austauschen,

und notfalls eigene Modelle trainieren oder zukaufen.

Mein klares Fazit
Die richtige Vision ist nicht:

„Eine KI macht aus MP3s neue Tracks.“

Die richtige Vision ist:

Ein terminal-basiertes Performance-Werkzeug, das jedes Eingangsstück in eine spielbare Triade aus MC-202, W-30 und TR-909 übersetzt — mit einer KI als sichtbar agierendem Mitspieler.

Das ist originell.
Das ist technisch machbar.
Das ist live spannend.
Und vor allem: das ist ein Produkt mit Charakter.
